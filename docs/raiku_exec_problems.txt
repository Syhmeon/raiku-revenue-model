Executive Summary
The Problem: Probabilistic Inclusion
Transaction inclusion on Solana is inherently probabilistic. When you submit a transaction, multiple factors determine whether it lands: network delivery, leader scheduling, execution validity, and finality. Under contention, the composite probability of successful inclusion drops significantly.
Empirical evidence:
Bot transactions experience ~58% failure rates vs ~6% for human users during normal conditions [1]
During high contention, failure rates can exceed 75% [2]
For PropAMMs, failed oracle updates lead to stale quotes, adverse selection, and lost market share

Real-world impact: HumidiFi, once commanding 26% of PropAMM market share, dropped to <1% in 10 weeks when execution reliability degraded. The market is unforgiving.

The Stakes: $287B Market at Risk
The cost of unreliable execution compounds: infrastructure spend on retries, wasted priority fees, stale quotes leading to adverse selection, and ultimately lost market share to more reliable competitors.


The Solution: Raiku's AOT Model
Raiku's Ahead-of-Time (AOT) blockspace reservation fundamentally changes the inclusion equation.


Key insight: AOT dramatically improves Delivery and Scheduling by eliminating real-time priority competition. You reserve capacity for a specific future slot, then submit when ready. The race shifts from "who gets included?" to "who detects and builds fastest?"—a race sophisticated traders can win consistently.

Section 1: The Problem
1.1 The Probabilistic Nature of Transaction Inclusion
Transaction inclusion on Solana depends on four independent factors:
P(Inclusion) = P(Delivery) × P(Scheduling) × P(Execution) × P(Finality)
P(Delivery): The probability your transaction reaches the target leader.
Affected by: Stake-Weighted QoS (SWQoS) rate limits, network latency, leader rotation timing
Without stake-backed routes, packets can be dropped before reaching the leader [3]

P(Scheduling): Given delivery, the probability of being scheduled for execution.
Leaders maintain finite buffers (TOTAL_BUFFERED_PACKETS = 100,000 in Agave) [4]
Transactions compete on priority: Priority = (Fee + Base) / (1 + CU + Signature CUs + Write Lock CUs) [5]
Account contention (AccountInUse) causes deferals

P(Execution): Given scheduling, the probability of successful execution.
Depends on transaction validity: slippage tolerances, state freshness, blockhash validity
This factor is largely independent of submission method

P(Finality): Given execution, the probability of achieving desired commitment.
processed → confirmed → finalized progression
Rollback risk exists until finalized (~12.8s currently; ~150ms post-Alpenglow) [6]

Empirical Baseline
Under contention with direct TPU submission:
P(Delivery) ≈ 0.70 — SWQoS and timing reduce delivery
P(Scheduling) ≈ 0.60 — Priority competition and buffer limits
P(Execution) ≈ 0.95 — Most valid transactions execute
P(Finality) ≈ 0.99 — Rollbacks are rare

Composite: P(Inclusion) ≈ 0.40

This aligns with empirical data showing ~58% bot transaction failure rates [1].
1.2 Why This Matters for PropAMMs
PropAMMs rely on frequent oracle updates to keep on-chain prices aligned with market prices. When updates fail:
Quotes go stale — The pricing curve drifts from true market price
Adverse selection increases — Informed traders pick off stale quotes
Aggregators route elsewhere — Jupiter and other routers prefer fresh quotes
Market share erodes — Volume flows to more reliable competitors

Adverse Selection Cost Framework
Adverse Selection Cost = Position Size × Price Move × P(Stale) × P(Fill|Stale)
This framework derives from market microstructure theory [7]. The key insight: failure probability clusters during volatility—exactly when price moves are largest and adverse selection is most costly.

Section 2: The Raiku Solution
2.1 Core Value Proposition
Raiku's value is not about being faster than direct TPU. It's about materially improving P(Inclusion) so your speed advantages convert to wins.
With AOT blockspace reservations:
P(Delivery) improves: siQoS path delivers directly to target leader
P(Scheduling) improves: Pre-reserved CUs eliminate priority competition

The race shifts from "who gets included first?" to "who detects and builds the transaction first?"—reflecting TradFi execution dynamics where infrastructure and strategy differentiate winners.
2.2 Ahead-of-Time (AOT) Execution
AOT turns blockspace into a schedulable resource. You pre-purchase slot-level inclusion commitments for a specific future slot, then submit through Raiku when needed.
Mechanics:
Bid for future-slot compute (CUs) at a price per CU via SubmitAotBid [I1]
Auction allocates by price_per_cu until CU cap reached [I3]
On submission, siQoS path routes to target leader's Sidecar [I10]
Optimistic failure mode: breaches are penalized [I7]

Important limitation: "Guaranteed inclusion" means slot-level inclusion commitment, not profit. Transactions can still fail if invalid or built against stale state.

Section 3: Competitive Landscape
3.1 Comparison Matrix

Key insight: Jito and Harmonic optimize how you compete in real-time auctions. Raiku lets you exit the auction for reserved capacity.
3.2 Jito
Dominant MEV infrastructure on Solana. Launched Block Assembly Marketplace (BAM) in September 2025, introducing PBS with TEE-based ordering.
The Block Engine runs parallel auctions at 50ms ticks. Bundles with intersecting locking patterns (write-write, read-write, write-read) compete in a single auction, while non-intersecting bundles run separately. Within each auction, bundles are ordered by tip-per-CU efficiency—not absolute tip—so compute-efficient transactions gain priority at lower cost. The engine simulates bundles to determine profitable combinations and submits the highest-paying set to validators up to a CU limit [11].
BAM introduces Proposer-Builder Separation (PBS) to Solana. BAM Nodes ingest transactions in Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs), sequence according to programmatic rules, and send to BAM Validators for execution. This architecture enables custom scheduling logic through Plugins—interfaces that allow applications to implement features like speed bumps or priority ordering without modifying consensus. Validators retain the option to use Agave, Firedancer, or other clients—BAM is opt-in [12].
Strengths: Network effects, established tip economy, institutional backing ($50M A16z, Oct 2025 [9])
Limitation: JIT-only—you still compete in real-time tip-per-CU auctions.

3.3 Harmonic
Block-building aggregation layer backed by Paradigm ($6M seed, Nov 2025 [10]). Operated by Temporal, parent of HumidiFi.
Harmonic creates an open marketplace where validators source blocks from multiple competing builders (Jito, Temporal, BAM, Paladin, etc.) rather than accepting a single "all-or-nothing" block. The system consists of: (1) TPU Relayers that aggregate transactions, (2) Block Builders that independently construct candidate blocks using their own strategies, (3) an Auction Engine that aggregates, verifies, normalizes, and scores builder blocks, and (4) Validators that apply configurable preferences (max revenue, compliance rules, anti-sandwich) to select blocks each slot [13].
Harmonic's thesis is that "fast and fair markets outperform fast-but-closed systems." By enabling builder competition, the system pushes value to validators and the network rather than a single intermediary. Validators can configure "preference presets" to defend users from toxic MEV flows while keeping markets efficient. Builder diversity also reduces correlated failures and lock-in risk—if one builder goes down, validators seamlessly receive blocks from others.
Strengths: Deep packing optimization, HumidiFi operational experience, multi-builder competition
Limitation: Still JIT competition, no scheduled blockspace

Section 4: Solana Protocol Evolution
4.1 Alpenglow (Expected Early-Mid 2026)
Replaces PoH + Tower BFT with Votor (consensus) and Rotor (propagation).
Key change: Finality drops from ~12.8s to 100–150ms [6]
Implication for Raiku: The gap between "scheduled" and "finalized" shrinks dramatically. AOT commitments become near-instant settlement.
4.2 Future Developments
Multiple Concurrent Leaders (MCL): Parallel block production for non-conflicting transactions
Asynchronous Execution: Ordering separated from execution

Both developments are compatible with—and potentially enhance—the AOT model.
